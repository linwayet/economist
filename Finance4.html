英文摘要 (English Summary)
Defence stocks have surged to historic valuations amid the American-Israeli war against Iran and a broader global rearmament trend. Western defence firms now trade at approximately 35 times forecast earnings — levels comparable to Nvidia — and some companies like Hanwha Aerospace and Kraken Robotics have seen their market values multiply more than ten-fold since early 2024. NATO members have collectively pledged to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP, raising hopes of an arms-buying bonanza.

However, the article cautions that these elevated valuations rest on a precarious Goldilocks scenario: investors need *just enough* conflict to sustain government procurement, but not so much that states resort to windfall taxes or contract renegotiation. Historical precedent is sobering — during both World Wars and the Korean War, the British and American governments imposed steep windfall taxes and repeatedly renegotiated contracts downward. Today, political signals echo history: Donald Trump has already issued executive orders restricting shareholder payouts by large defence contractors, and European Greens are advocating windfall taxes. The article concludes that while armsmakers are the corporate stars of a dangerous world, their investors face genuine downside risk if geopolitical conflict escalates beyond the "just right" threshold.

隨著武裝衝突不斷蔓延，投資者對武器供應商的熱情不難理解。然而，這其中潛藏著風險。這些股票正處於近代史上最貴的估值水準。西方國防企業的股票以約35倍的預期盈利交易，與Nvidia相差無幾——而後者的人工智慧晶片銷售每年增長75%。這些估值倍數唯有在戰鼓持續擂響的前提下才說得通——但前提是不能響得太響，以至於政府限制企業獲利能力。
面對美國退出盟友關係的前景，北約所有成員國都承諾將國防開支提升至GDP的3.5%。這可能帶來一波軍備採購熱潮。但這並非必然，尤其是如果全球局勢趨於平靜。當軍事開支與退休金、醫療保健及其他受歡迎的福利政策相互競爭時，政客們最終可能選擇「奶油」而非「大砲」。
即便政府真的放開預算，許多國家達成國防支出目標的方式，未必是也未必主要是購買更多戰機、坦克和飛彈。其中部分增量不會流向軍事裝備，而是用於軍事退休金。另一部分則會投入那些只有瞇眼看才算得上國防相關的項目。義大利政府曾希望將連接西西里島與大陸的大橋列為重要軍事資產，但在美國大使介入後便打消了這個念頭。
對投資者而言，比「國防預算增加可能是海市蜃樓」更大的威脅，是政府當真了。軍火商幾乎完全依賴國家採購。這使他們在危機時期成為被強取豪奪的對象——甚至面臨徵用的風險。
在兩次世界大戰期間，英國政府以高額暴利稅將軍火商的利潤悉數收走。1942年美國全面投入二戰後，政府一再「重新談判」此前與武器公司簽訂的合約——每次都是向下調整，且幅度極大。韓戰期間政府故技重施，此後在對抗蘇聯的冷戰過程中一直延續至1970年代末。

- Windfall Tax（暴利稅/超額利潤稅）： * 定義：政府對因外部環境（而非企業自身努力）獲得異常高額利潤的企業徵收的特別稅，通常是一次性或限期徵收，稅率高於正常企業所得稅。 * 文中脈絡：文章以二戰時英國對軍火商徵收暴利稅為歷史佐證，並指出今日歐洲議會綠黨正呼籲對獲利豐厚的國防企業重新採用此政策工具。 * 重要性：暴利稅代表了「政治風險」（political risk）的核心形式——即政府以立法手段追回企業從政府採購中賺取的超額利潤，直接侵蝕股東回報。 * 相關概念：Excess Profits Tax（超額利潤稅）、Resource Rent Tax（資源租金稅）、Solidarity Levy（連帶稅） * 實例：2022年英國推出能源暴利稅（Energy Profits Levy），對北海油氣生產商徵收高達25%的額外稅，後升至35%；歐盟也在同年對電力企業推出類似措施。
- Capital Return（資本回報）：股票回購（Share Buyback）與股利（Dividend）： * 定義：企業將盈利分配給股東的兩種主要方式。股票回購（buyback）指企業在公開市場購回自身股票，減少流通股數，提高每股盈利；股利（dividend）指直接向股東發放現金。 * 文中脈絡：川普行政命令明確禁止大型國防承包商進行股票回購和發放股利，試圖阻止政府採購資金透過資本回報流入股東口袋。 * 重要性：資本回報政策是機構投資者評估公司吸引力的核心指標；禁止回購和股利等同於降低了國防股對投資者的財務誘因，直接影響估值。 * 相關概念：Total Shareholder Return（股東總回報）、Yield（收益率）、Payout Ratio（配息率） * 實例：美國國防巨頭洛克希德·馬丁（Lockheed Martin）歷年股票回購金額高達數十億美元，是機構投資者的重要持股理由之一。

Why war isn’t always good for defence stocks They win only if governments want just enough weapons—but not too many
第 39 頁
彩色多頁排版主題：奶茶暖調
Financial markets are never more ghoulish than in times of bloodshed. As soon as a missile is fired somewhere in the world, they start gauging not just losses but potential opportunities for profit. Even for believers in the efficiency of capitalism, the process is icky to behold. It is again on display amid the American-Israeli war against Iran. The obvious winners (besides energy firms benefiting from surging oil and gas prices) are armsmakers. On March 2nd American defence stocks rose by 3%. In Europe, firms like bae Systems and Hensoldt bucked a broader equity sell-off. Across the rich world, weapons-makers are up by 52% over the past 12 months. Some have done much better. Hanwha Aerospace of South Korea and Kraken Robotics, a Canadian maker of sonar, have increased their market value more than ten-fold since the start of 2024. In venture capital, defence seems as hot as artificial intelligence. With armed conflicts spreading, investors’ enthusiasm for arms suppliers is understandable. Yet it comes with risks. The stocks are at their priciest in modern history. Western defence firms’ shares trade at around 35 times their forecast earnings, not far off Nvidia, whose sales of the ai boom’s favoured chips are growing by 75% a year. These multiples make sense only if the drumbeat of war persists—but is not so loud that governments crimp the firms’ ability to generate profits. Faced with the prospect of America retreating from its alliances, members of nato have all promised to raise defence spending to 3.5% of gdp. This could translate into an arms-buying bonanza. But it does not have to, especially if things around the world calm down. When military spending clashes with pensions, health care and other popular giveaways, politicians may end up choosing butter over guns. If they do loosen the purse-strings, then many governments are likely to meet their defence-spending targets not only, or even mainly, by buying more fighter jets, tanks and missiles. Some of the increase will not go on materiel but on military pensions. Some will be invested in things that look defence-related only if you squint. The Italian government hoped to classify a bridge connecting Sicily to the mainland as a critical military asset, but backtracked after a talking-to from the American ambassador. An even bigger threat to investors than the prospect of rising defence budgets proving illusory is that governments are dead serious. Armsmakers cater almost exclusively to states. This turns them into targets for strong-arming in periods of crisis—or even for expropriation. During the two world wars the British government hoovered up the profits of armaments manufacturers with steep windfall taxes. After America fully entered the second world war in 1942 its government repeatedly “renegotiated” prices previously agreed in contracts with weapons firms—always downwards and by a lot. It did the same again during the Korean war, and continued until the late 1970s as it fought the cold war against the Soviet Union. For example, the shares of America’s aircraft manufacturers did well from 1938 until the attack on Pearl Harbour in December 1941, according to Stephen Ciccone and Fred Kaen of the University of New Hampshire. But from then until its end in 1945 a broad portfolio of American equities offered a higher return. Now that armsmakers are again turning lucrative, politicians are sizing them up for squeezing. In January Donald Trump issued an executive order prohibiting large American defence contractors from buying back shares or paying dividends. He also mused that their bosses’ annual pay should be capped at $5m (a quarter of what a typical one got in 2024). The order and the musing lack legal power but send a clear message. Across the Atlantic and the political spectrum, Greens in the European Parliament are calling for a windfall tax on profitable defence firms to claw back the fruits of public spending. Between the war in Ukraine, now in its fifth year, the one raging in the skies over the Middle East, and another that could erupt if China decides to invade Taiwan, armsmakers are enjoying the limelight more than at any time in decades. They are some of the biggest corporate winners in a more dangerous, unstable world. But their continued success is contingent on a Goldilocks scenario of just enough conflict but not too much. Otherwise their investors may find returns to be thin gruel. ■
