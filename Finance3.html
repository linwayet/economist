Section 1｜文章摘要 Article Summary
英文摘要 (English Summary)
Europe faces a deepening retirement crisis driven by demographics: with a median citizen aged 45 and fewer than three workers per pensioner, pension obligations consume roughly one fifth of EU GDP. Yet the very systems straining public finances also represent an enormous unrealised opportunity for capital markets. America manages $43 trillion in pension assets — nearly 140% of GDP — while the EU has just over $5 trillion, less than 30% of GDP.
第 1 頁
彩色多頁排版主題：奶茶暖調
Most European pensions operate on "pay-as-you-go" (PAYG) systems: worker contributions finance current pensioners, leaving almost nothing invested. In Germany, France, Italy, and Spain, such schemes cover more than 90% of employees and run deficits ranging from 2% to 6% of GDP. The exceptions — the Netherlands, Denmark, and Sweden — demonstrate that fully funded systems can work: their pension assets rival or exceed America's as a share of GDP, and at least 20% is invested in equities, supporting vibrant domestic capital markets.
The article argues that if all EU countries matched the American benchmark of pension assets at 140% of GDP, the total would reach $30 trillion. If a quarter were in equities and a fifth of that stayed in Europe, €1.5 trillion in fresh capital would flow to European companies — a transformative sum compared with the $18 trillion current market capitalisation of the Stoxx 600.

退休金、醫療保健及老年長期照護方面的公共支出，相當於歐盟GDP的五分之一，且仍在持續增加。每當有人提出削減退休金的方案，無不遭到老齡選民的強烈反對。大量資源因此從國防、基礎設施等重要投資領域被轉移走。
換言之，退休金對歐盟而言既是龐大的經濟負擔，同時卻也是一個巨大的浪費機遇。美國退休基金管理的資產規模達43兆美元，相當於GDP的近140%。而整個歐盟的數字僅略超過5兆美元，不到GDP的30%。若能提高這一比例，將對歐洲發育不良的資本市場帶來翻天覆地的改變。
在許多歐洲國家，大多數退休金由公共計畫提供，而這些計畫大多採用「隨收隨付」制（pay-as-you-go）。工作者薪資中扣除的繳費，被用來支付當前退休人員的養老金。幾乎沒有資金被用於投資。在德國、法國、義大利和西班牙，這些制度覆蓋了90%以上的雇員。據波士頓諮詢集團（Boston Consulting Group）數據，這些制度運作著大量赤字，需要政府以GDP的2%（德國）至6%（義大利）的規模來填補。

荷蘭一些大型職業退休基金的投資組合中，約有一半資產配置在歐洲。AP4是支持瑞典隨收隨付制的基金之一，其投資組合中有15%配置在瑞典股票、9%在瑞典公債，部分原因是為了使資產與瑞典克朗計價的負債相匹配，另外也受到偏重國內投資的授權指引所驅動。丹麥基金的股票持倉中，雖然只有5%至10%是本國股票，但這仍然使其對本土股市的曝險比例偏高——丹麥股市僅佔全球股票市值的0.5%。
從歷史上看，將投資組合過重配置於本土市場，使歐洲退休基金未能充分受惠於其他地區——尤其是美國——股票市場的飆升。然而，現在這個計算正在改變。
一方面，歐洲股票近來已跑贏美國股票。自2025年初以來，代表歐洲大型企業的Stoxx 600指數以美元計算已上漲36%，而大西洋彼岸的同類指數S&P 500僅上漲15%。相對於盈利而言，美國公司的估值已顯偏高，這可能壓低未來回報；而許多歐洲公司目前相對仍具估值吸引力。歐洲科技企業終於也開始嶄露頭角，這使其成為退休基金在多元配置創投和其他另類資產時更具吸引力的投資標的。
要將法國、德國等地根深蒂固的退休金制度改革成類似丹麥、荷蘭和瑞典的模式，並非易事。但改革帶來的獎賞相當可觀。如果歐盟所有國家的退休金資產都能達到相當於GDP 140%的美國水準，這些資金池將持有近30兆美元資產。若其中四分之一投資於股票（反映丹麥、荷蘭和瑞典基金的持股比例），且其中五分之一留在歐洲，流向歐洲企業的可用資本池將增加1.5兆美元。相較之下，Stoxx 600目前的市值為18兆美元。歐洲依然年邁，但至少將更加矯健。

- Pay-as-You-Go (PAYG) vs. Fully Funded Pension System（隨收隨付制 vs. 完全提撥制）： * 定義：退休金制度的兩種基本模式。「隨收隨付」（PAYG）指現任工作者的繳費直接支付給現任退休者，無資本積累；「完全提撥」（fully funded）指每位工作者為自己的退休金預先儲蓄並進行投資，形成資本儲備。 * 文中脈絡：文章的核心論點是歐洲大多數國家採用PAYG制度，而荷蘭、丹麥、瑞典等少數國家已成功轉型為完全提撥或混合制，後者創造了龐大的可投資資本池。 * 重要性：這一制度差異直接決定了退休金能否轉化為長期投資資本——PAYG制度下的退休金是純粹的「移轉支付」，不進入資本市場；完全提撥制下的退休金則是機構投資的長線資金來源。 * 相關概念：Notional Defined Contribution（名義確定提撥制，NDC，瑞典模式的一部分）、Buffer Fund（緩衝基金）、三支柱退休金體系（第一支柱：國家退休金；第二支柱：職業退休金；第三支柱：個人退休金） * 實例：荷蘭的APG集團（代表多個大型職業退休基金，資產超過7,000億歐元）是全球完全提撥制最成功的案例，其多元化投資組合涵蓋全球股票、房地產、基礎設施和私募股權。
- Capital Markets Union（資本市場聯盟，CMU）： * 定義：歐盟委員會（European Commission）自2015年提出的一項重大計畫，旨在整合歐盟27個成員國分散的資本市場，創建類似於美國的深度統一資本市場，使企業更易於跨境融資。 * 文中脈絡：本文的退休金改革建議，實質上是為CMU提供了一個關鍵的「供給側」解決方案——通過創造大規模的機構長線資本，填充歐洲資本市場的「深度缺口」。 * 重要性：CMU被視為歐盟提升競爭力的核心經濟議程，但十年來進展緩慢，主要障礙是各國稅法、破產法和金融監管的差異；退休金制度的統一改革可能是突破CMU瓶頸的關鍵槓桿。 * 相關概念：歐盟單一市場（Single Market）、歐洲資本市場（European Capital Markets）、泛歐養老金產品（Pan-European Personal Pension Product, PEPP） * 實例：德拉吉報告（2024年Mario Draghi報告）將CMU列為歐洲提升生產力和創新競爭力的五大優先政策之一。

Finance & economics | Oldies’ goodies
European pensions are a $30trn missed opportunity If only more countries went Dutch
第 39 頁
彩色多頁排版主題：奶茶暖調
Europe is known as the “old continent” because it is not the “new world”. From ancient Greek democracy to classical music, it is the cradle of Western civilisation. These days a less charitable contrast is not with the new but with the young. The median citizen of the European Union is 45, six years senior to an equivalent American and four to a Chinese person. The eu already has fewer than three residents of working age for every pensioner. Public spending on retirement benefits, health and long-term care for the elderly is equivalent to a fifth of the bloc’s gdp, and counting. Proposed cuts to pensions are invariably met with fierce opposition from ageing voters. Resources are diverted away from important investments in sectors like defence and infrastructure. Pensions are, in other words, a huge economic liability for the eu. At the same time, however, they are a gigantic wasted opportunity. America’s pension funds manage $43trn in assets, equivalent to nearly 140% of gdp. In the eu as a whole, the figure is just over $5trn, less than 30% of gdp. Increasing it could do wonders for Europe’s stunted capital markets. In many European countries, most pensions are furnished by public schemes and most of these are “pay-as-you-go”. Contributions are deducted from workers’ wages in the form of a payroll tax and used to finance payments to current pensioners. Precious little is invested. In Germany, France, Italy and Spain, these systems cover more than 90% of employees. They run up large deficits that governments must plug, from 2% of gdp in Germany to 6% of gdp in Italy, according to the Boston Consulting Group. Occupy Wall Street, please! Some occupational schemes exist, where employees set aside part of their paycheques. But these are small and, because they often guarantee minimum payments, a lot of the proceeds are invested in safe government bonds. In the eu’s four biggest economies only about a fifth of the money in such schemes finds its way into capital markets. Individual pension plans (which tend to get favourable tax treatment) are similarly conservative—and often charge eye-watering fees which eat into already meagre returns.

chart: the economist A few outliers show that things do not have to be this way. In proportion to gdp, Sweden’s pension assets are roughly as large as America’s. Those of the Netherlands and Denmark are higher (see chart). At least 20% of those three countries’ pension portfolios are invested in equities, with the rest mostly sitting in government bonds and other less racy assets. A lot of this equity capital flows to local businesses, which helps explain why the three countries have some of Europe’s most vibrant stockmarkets. Some large Dutch occupational funds have around half their portfolios parked in European assets. The ap4, one of the funds that backs Sweden’s pay-as-you-go system, invests 15% in Swedish equities and 9% in Swedish bonds, partly to match assets with Swedish krona liabilities, and partly because of mandates that favour domestic investments. Although only between 5% and 10% of Danish funds’ equity holdings are domestic, this still leaves them disproportionately exposed to the local stockmarket, which accounts for just 0.5% of global market capitalisation. Historically, investing an outsize slice of their portfolios at home prevented European pension funds from taking advantage of soaring stockmarkets elsewhere, especially in America. Now, though, the calculus is changing. For one thing, European equities have outperformed American ones lately. Since the start of 2025 the stoxx 600 index of large European firms has risen by 36% in dollar terms, compared with 15% for the s&p 500, its transatlantic cousin. American companies are already richly valued relative to earnings, which may depress future returns, whereas many European ones still look relatively cheap. And European technology firms are at last coming into their own. This makes them more attractive investments for pension funds diversifying into venture capital and other alternative assets. Reforming sclerotic pension systems in places like France and Germany to resemble those in Denmark, the Netherlands and Sweden is not going to be easy. But the prize is considerable. If all eu countries had pension assets worth 140% of gdp, the American figure, then these pots would hold nearly $30trn. If a quarter was invested in equities, reflecting the share in Danish, Dutch and Swedish funds, and a fifth of that stayed in Europe, the pool of capital available to European companies would grow by $1.5trn. By comparison, the current market capitalisation of stoxx 600 is $18trn. Europe would still be old. But at least it would be more spry. ■
