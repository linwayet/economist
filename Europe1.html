Europe's technology sector is experiencing a remarkable revival, with companies like Lovable demonstrating rapid growth and proving that building world-class AI firms in Europe is now feasible. Historically lagging behind America and China, Europe has faced challenges including fragmented markets, regulatory barriers, and limited venture capital access. However, multiple factors are now favoring European tech: deteriorating US-Europe relations have prompted policymakers to strengthen the technology ecosystem; Trump's policies are making Europe more attractive to tech workers and investors; China's state-directed innovation model is redirecting venture capital westward; and established European tech tycoons are now mentoring younger entrepreneurs. Venture capital investment in European startups surged to $85 billion last year, up from $22 billion a decade earlier. Three sectors are particularly promising: climate technology, defense technology, and deep-tech innovations. While Europe is unlikely to replace America as the dominant tech power, it is becoming increasingly competitive in specific areas. The transformation reflects both structural economic changes and geopolitical shifts that have fundamentally altered the continent's technological trajectory.

Lovable 在斯德哥爾摩的辦公室裝潢與這家新創公司舒適的名字相符。Lovable 專門從事「氛圍編程」——提示人工智能系統製作軟體——實施脫鞋政策。瑞典咖啡的香氣瀰漫在空氣中。帶有公司標誌的心形抱枕放在舒適的沙發上。
但 Lovable 不如看起來那麼溫柔可愛，而是靈活且具有競爭力。它規模小，但增長迅速：今年一月，其年度經常性收入達到 3 億美元，比 14 個月前的 100 萬美元增長了。共同創辦人 Anton Osika 主張，在歐洲建立一家世界級的 AI 公司現在是可能的。他說：「思想轉變正在發生。」
歐洲長期以來在創造科技巨頭方面一直是落後者。如今歐洲（即歐盟、英國和挪威）僅是世界上 100 家最有價值科技公司中的 6 家所在地。美國有 56 家；中國有 16 家。這片大陸的缺點眾所周知。其 5.2 億人口的市場被語言和監管制度分割。它擁有大量人才，得益於其世界級的研究機構和大學。但其企業家在快速擴展公司方面一直在籌集資本上遇到困難。
但舊大陸出現了新的希望。由於與美國關係的惡化，其政策制定者正在加倍努力加強其技術生態系統。與此同時，美國和中國採取的決策使歐洲對科技工作者和投資者相對更具吸引力。其現有科技公司雖然數量較少，但現在正在培育新一代新創企業。去年風險投資在歐洲新創企業的投資達到 850 億美元，比十年前的 220 億美元增加了。儘管沉迷於 AI 的美國仍然遠領先，去年投資了 3,390 億美元，但中國現在落後，僅有 530 億美元。
2024 年，義大利前總理馬里奧·德拉基在一份引人注目的報告中批評歐洲競爭力下降。但正是唐納德·川普，以其對該地區的敵意，真正激勵了政策制定者。他們現在將歐洲的技術缺陷視為地緣政治風險以及商業風險。

歐洲委員會科技沙皇 Henna Virkkunen 表示，委員會正在研究方法以鼓勵歐盟政府從本土新創企業購買更多技術。例如，歐洲企業也正在「認識到他們不能完全依賴外國供應商」，French AI 模型製造商 Mistral 的老闆 Arthur Mensch 表示。
也許更重要的是，政策制定者正在採取措施幫助企業家建立他們的業務。本月委員會即將公佈一項計畫，統一歐洲支離破碎的資本市場，這將幫助新創企業籌集資金。這不會很快，因為它需要做出有關協調各國稅務制度的艱難選擇。各成員國也在升級其資本市場，這對此有幫助。英國、法國和德國正在調整規則，以鼓勵養老基金投資更多風險資產，例如年輕科技公司。
與此同時，川普先生對外國人的蔑視，以及美國科技巨頭最近的裁員，正在驅趕人才流向歐洲。在美國公司為歐洲工作的人員也形成了人才庫。來自 Revelio Labs（一家職場數據公司）的數據顯示，人才流失已經逆轉。Lovable 就招聘了美國軟體公司的高管。更重要的是，越來越少的歐洲公司被出售給美國。數據提供商 Dealogic 指出，在 2011-13 年，美國公司按數量計佔歐洲科技公司收購的 12%，按價值計佔 35%。在 2023-25 年，這些比例為 9% 和 17%。
中國也無意中提供了幫助。其國家主導創新模式已經擠出了私人投資並縮減了風險投資支出，導致部分資金流向歐洲。在 2015 年至 2025 年間，中國在全球風險投資支出中的份額從 30% 下降到 10%。歐洲的比例從 12% 增加到 16%。
歐洲也正在克服其讓技術人員賺取大量金錢的不願。在去年出版的《創新的新地理》中，世界經濟論壇的 Mehran Gul 指出，Skype（一家歐洲新創企業）在 2000 年代初期僅創造了 11 位百萬富翁。PayPal（一家美國企業）向其員工提供了更多股票期權，創造了 100 多位百萬富翁。他們反過來又在較新的矽谷新創企業中進行了投資。

彩色多頁排版主題：奶茶暖調
現在歐洲科技公司正在發放更多期權，該地區建立的科技大亨正在幫助年輕人發家致富。Revolut（一家金融科技公司）的創辦人 Nikolay Storonsky 支持了 Spiko（一家法國同行業新創企業）和 Biorce（一家西班牙醫療技術公司）。Spotify 創辦人 Daniel Ek 是德國國防科技公司 Helsing 的大投資者。根據另一家數據提供商 Dealroom 和風險投資基金 Accel 的數據，Klarna（瑞典金融科技明星）的前員工創造了 60 多家新創企業。
這都不意味著歐洲將取代美國成為領先的技術大國。去年，它在世界 94 個新的大型語言模型中只推出了 2 個，根據人工智能研究機構 Epoch AI 的數據。歐盟到 2030 年製造世界五分之一計算機芯片的想法是荒謬的。但在某些領域，歐洲變得更具競爭力。三個部門引人注目，它們都得到了川普行動的幫助。
即使在他去年開始第二任期之前，歐洲的氣候技術部門也在追趕美國。在 2015-16 年，歐洲綠色新創企業的風險投資支出是美國的 24%。到 2024-25 年，這一比例增長到 55%。川普對美國環保法規的徹底廢除肯定會鼓勵這一趨勢。去年，籌集風險資金的美國氣候科技新創企業數量是 2019 年以來最低的。
歐洲綠色科技公司中沒有沮喪的跡象。今年 12 月，British 綠色電力供應商 Octopus Energy 分拆了出售智能電網軟體的 Kraken，估值為 90 億美元。瑞典是綠色科技新創企業的熱點。Stegra 的目標是在那裡製造無碳鋼。Einride 正在實現貨運電氣化。在瑞士，Climeworks 製造從空氣中吸收二氧化碳的機器。
川普要求歐洲更多地自衛的要求也刺激了該地區幾乎沒有的高科技武器製造。在 2015-17 年，歐洲國防科技的風險投資幾乎只有北美的 1%。到 2023-25 年，這已上升到 6%。國際戰略研究所（一家研究機構）表示，歐洲的國防支出從 2023 年至 2025 年增長了 42%；美國的國防預算雖然要大得多，但保持不變。而美國國防支出的大部分由建立的承包商佔據，歐洲為年輕國防科技公司提供了更多的機會。

慕尼黑已成為一個樞紐。在這座城市 Helsing 總部後面標有「機密」的金屬板門後面是一個「演示室」，其最新的武器以紅燈照亮。HX-2 是一種機翼呈 X 形、範圍為 100 公里的無人機，配備了 AI 系統，即使目標受到通信幹擾技術的保護，它也可以幫助其攻擊目標，如坦克。
Helsing 堅定不移地是歐洲的。它稱其正在建造的自主戰鬥機為「歐羅巴」。大部分資本來自 Ek 先生，使公司不太依賴美國風險投資。Helsing 不想成為「美國某個更大事物的小兄弟」，共同創辦人 Niklas Köhler 說。其鄰近公司包括監視無人機製造商 Quantum Systems（估值 40 億美元）和 Isar Aerospace（製造發射小型衛星的運載工具）。當烏克蘭的創新無人機製造商完成與俄羅斯的戰鬥後，許多人可能會創建或加入歐洲國防科技公司。
投資未經證實技術的「深度科技」公司也可能從川普對科學研究的敵意中受益。從慕尼黑馬克斯·普朗克等離子物理研究所分拆出來的 Proxima Fusion 已為核聚變反應堆籌集了超過 2 億歐元（2.36 億美元）。附近是量子計算新創企業，例如也是馬克斯·普朗克分拆的 Planqc，以及專門從事奈米技術、光子學和激光通信的公司。
歐洲風險投資流向深度科技公司的份額從 2021 年的 19% 上升到 2025 年的 36%，根據風險投資基金 Atomico 的計算。在某些細分市場，歐洲新創企業籌集的資金超過了美國。自 2023 年以來，歐洲氫氣新創企業（其技術既是深度又是綠色）已獲得了比美國年輕公司更多的資金。在量子技術方面，歐洲人和美國人幾乎處於同等地位。
歐洲科技曙光可能會被證明是虛假的。一個擔憂，特別是對國防公司而言，是政府現金短缺。2月 25 日，德國立法機構的預算委員會要求「節制」國防支出，並削減了與 Helsing 和競爭對手 Stark Defence 簽訂合同的支出。很少有人認為世界下一個萬億美元科技巨頭將是歐洲的。但也許第一次，這不是一個愚蠢的想法。

歷史背景：為什麼歐洲一直落後
歐洲在科技創新方面的落後並非最近現象，而是從20世紀末就開始形成的長期趨勢。當互聯網和個人電腦革命在1990年代展開時，美國憑藉其強大的創業文化、充裕的風險投資、統一的大型市場和優越的人才吸引力，迅速建立了世界科技領導地位。相比之下，歐洲面臨的障礙是結構性的：27個歐盟成員國各自獨立的法律體系、稅收制度和監管框架，導致資本市場支離破碎。語言差異進一步加強了市場分割。這些因素結合起來，使得歐洲新創企業難以快速擴展，且面臨更高的合規成本。

當前地緣政治環境：川普因素
2024年美國總統大選後，川普當選為第二任總統，這對歐洲科技生態產生了令人驚訝的積極影響。川普政府的一系列政策決定，包括對進口產品的關稅、對移民的限制性立場、對國際合作的懷疑態度，以及對環保規制的取消，實際上推動了歐洲科技的發展。
首先，川普政府對環保規制的削減使得歐洲的氣候技術優勢凸顯出來。歐洲在綠色能源和可持續發展上一直有領導地位，但在美國政府鼓勵綠色科技的時代，歐洲企業被迫與美國企業競爭。現在，隨著美國政府轉向，歐洲企業獲得了競爭優勢。
其次，川普政府對移民的蔑視和美國科技公司隨後的大規模裁員（如Meta裁員21%，Amazon計劃裁員1萬名員工），使得全球科技人才開始重新考慮他們的職業選擇。在川普時代移居美國變得更加困難，而歐洲各國（特別是那些提供技術簽證的國家）則變得更具吸引力。
第三，川普要求歐洲增加軍費開支以加強對俄羅斯的防禦，這刺激了歐洲國防科技的發展。歐洲國防支出在2023-2025年間增加了42%，這為歐洲國防科技公司創造了巨大的市場機會。

It is small, but growing fast: in January its annualised recurring revenue hit $300m, up from $1m 14 months earlier. Anton Osika, a co-founder, argues that building a world-beating ai company in Europe is now possible. “The mindset change is happening,” he says. Europe has long been a laggard in creating tech giants. Today Europe (ie, the European Union, Britain and Norway) is home to just six of the world’s 100 most valuable tech companies. America has 56; China, 16 (see chart 1). The continent’s drawbacks are well known. Its market of 520m people is divided by language and regulation. It has plenty of talent, thanks to its world-class research labs and universities. But its entrepreneurs have had trouble raising capital to expand their companies quickly.

彩色多頁排版主題：奶茶暖調
chart: the economist Yet there is new hope for the old continent. Jolted by the deterioration of its relationship with America, its policymakers are redoubling efforts to strengthen its technology ecosystem. Meanwhile, America and China have made decisions that make Europe relatively more attractive to tech workers and investors. Its established tech companies, though few, are now nurturing a new generation of startups. Last year venture-capital (vc) investments in European startups rose to $85bn, from $22bn a decade earlier (see chart 2). Although ai-mad America is still far ahead, investing $339bn last year, China now lags behind, with $53bn. In an attention-grabbing report in 2024 Mario Draghi, a former Italian prime minister, bashed Europe for its sagging competitiveness. But it is Donald Trump, with his animus towards the region, who has really galvanised policymakers. They now see Europe’s techno-failings as a geopolitical risk as well as a commercial one. Henna Virkkunen, the European Commission’s tech czar, says the commission is looking into ways to encourage eu governments to buy more technology from home-grown startups, for instance. European enterprises are also “realising that they can’t afford to entirely depend on foreign providers”, says Arthur Mensch, the boss of Mistral, a French maker of ai models. Perhaps more importantly, policymakers are taking steps to help entrepreneurs build up their businesses. This month the commission is due to publish a plan to unify Europe’s segmented capital markets, which will help startups raise money. That will not be quick, as it requires difficult choices about harmonising national tax regimes. It helps that member states are also upgrading their capital markets. Britain, France and Germany are tweaking rules to encourage pension funds to invest more in risky assets, such as young tech firms.

chart: the economist Meanwhile, Mr Trump’s disdain for foreigners, and recent lay-offs by American tech giants, are driving talent to Europe. People working in Europe for American firms have also formed a talent reservoir. Data from Revelio Labs, a workplace-data firm, show the brain drain has reversed (see chart 3). Lovable, for one, has recruited executives from American software companies. What is more, fewer European firms are being sold to America. Dealogic, a data provider, notes that in 2011-13 American firms made 12% of acquisitions of European tech firms by number and 35% by value. In 2023-25 the shares were 9% and 17%. China, too, is helping inadvertently. Its model of state-directed innovation has crowded out private investment and shrunk vc spending, pushing some towards Europe. Between 2015 and 2025 China’s share of global vc spending fell from 30% to 10%. Europe’s grew from 12% to 16%. Europe is also getting over its reluctance to let techies make lots of money. In “The New Geography of Innovation”, published last year, Mehran Gul of the World Economic Forum notes that Skype, a European startup, created just 11 millionaires in the early 2000s. PayPal, an American one, gave many more stock options to its employees, creating over 100. They, in turn, invested in newer Silicon Valley startups. Now European tech firms are giving out more options, and the region’s established tech tycoons are helping youngsters make fortunes. Nikolay Storonsky, founder of Revolut, a fintech company, has backed Spiko, a French startup in the same sector, and Biorce, a Spanish medical-technology firm. Daniel Ek, Spotify’s founder, is a big investor in Helsing, a German defence-tech firm. Former staff of Klarna, a Swedish fintech star, have created more than 60 startups, according to Dealroom, another data provider, and Accel, a vc fund. None of this means that Europe will supplant America as the leading techno-power. Last year it launched just two of the world’s 94 new large language models, according to Epoch ai, a think-tank. The idea that the eu will manufacture a fifth of the world’s computer chips by 2030, an objective of the commission, is fanciful. But in some areas Europe is becoming more competitive. Three sectors—all of them helped by Mr Trump’s actions—stand out. Even before he started his second term last year, Europe’s climate-technology sector was catching up with America’s. In 2015-16 vc spending on Europe’s green startups was 24% of America’s. By 2024-25 that ratio grew to 55%. Mr Trump’s gutting of American environmental regulation will surely encourage the trend. Last year the number of American climate-tech startups raising vc funds was the lowest since 2019. There is no sign of demoralisation among European green-tech firms. In December Octopus Energy, a British provider of green power, spun off Kraken, which sells smart-grid software, at an estimated valuation of $9bn. Sweden is a hotspot for green-tech startups. Stegra aims to make carbon-free steel there. Einride is electrifying freight transport. In Switzerland Climeworks builds machines that suck carbon dioxide from the air. Mr Trump’s demand that Europe do more to defend itself is also spurring high-tech arms-making in a region that had little of it. In 2015-17 vc investment in European defence tech was barely 1% of North America’s. By 2023-25 that had risen to 6%. The International Institute for Strategic Studies, a think-tank, says that Europe’s defence spending rose by 42% from 2023 to 2025; America’s defence budget, though far bigger, was unchanged. Whereas established contractors account for much of American defence spending, Europe offers more scope for young defence-tech firms. Munich has emerged as a hub for them. Behind a metal-panelled door marked “Confidential” at Helsing’s headquarters in the city is a “demo room”, where its newest weapons are illuminated with red light. The hx-2, a drone with wings in the shape of an X and a range of 100km, has ai systems that help it attack targets, such as tanks, even if they are protected by communications-jamming technology. Helsing is defiantly European. It calls the autonomous fighter it is building “Europa”. Much of the capital comes from Mr Ek, leaving the firm less reliant on American vcs. Helsing does not want to be “the little brother of some bigger thing in America”, says Niklas Köhler, a co-founder. Its neighbours include Quantum Systems, a maker of surveillance drones that is valued at $4bn, and Isar Aerospace, which builds vehicles that launch small satellites. When Ukraine’s innovative drone-makers are done fighting Russia, many may found or join European defence-tech firms. “Deep-tech” firms investing in unproven technologies may benefit too from Mr Trump’s hostility to scientific research. Proxima Fusion, spun off from the Max Planck Institute for Plasma Physics in Munich, has raised over €200m ($236m) for nuclear-fusion reactors. Nearby are quantum-computing startups, such as Planqc, also a Max Planck spinoff, and firms specialising in nanotechnology, photonics and laser communications. The share of European vc investment going to deep-tech firms rose from 19% in 2021 to 36% in 2025, calculates Atomico, a vc fund. In some niches Europe’s startups are raising more than America’s. Since 2023 European hydrogen startups (whose technology is both deep and green) have secured more capital than young American firms. In quantum technologies, Europeans and Americans are almost on a par. It is possible that Europe’s tech dawn will prove false. One worry, especially for defence firms, is that governments are short of cash. On February 25th the budget committee of Germany’s legislature called for “moderation” in defence spending and cut the expenditure on contracts with Helsing and Stark Defence, a rival. Few think the world’s next trillion-dollar tech giant will be European. But, perhaps for the first time, it is not a silly idea. ■
