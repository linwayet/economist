英文摘要 (English Summary)
India's Ministry of Statistics released revised GDP figures on February 27th, 2026, implementing the country's first methodological update since 2015. The revision reset the base year from 2011 to 2022 and incorporated new data sources, producing a somewhat sobering result: India's GDP is 3.3% smaller than previously estimated. The country therefore fell short of its ambition, projected in a December 2025 government report, of overtaking Japan as the world's fourth-largest economy.

Yet the revision also carries encouraging news: India's GDP growth rate was revised upward to 7.1% for fiscal year 2024-25, from an earlier estimate of 6.5%, cementing its status as the world's fastest-growing major economy. The sectoral breakdown paints a more rural picture — agriculture, especially fisheries and dairy, is larger than thought, while commerce, hotels and transport are 26% smaller. The service sector overall shrank by 8% on the new methodology, now comprising 41% of the economy. Manufacturing (15% of GDP) also contracted slightly, missing Narendra Modi's target of reaching 25% by 2025.
The revision also reassures observers about the integrity of Indian statistics: previous controversies — including doubts about post-2014 GDP revisions and the shelving of a 2019 consumer survey showing rural consumption drops — had raised suspicions of data manipulation. New, less-flattering numbers signal that India's statistical agencies are willing to report uncomfortable truths.

India’s economy is not as big as economists thought But it is growing faster
第 38 頁
彩色多頁排版主題：奶茶暖調
Indian officials have been in a boastful mood lately. A government report in December argued that judging by real-time economic indicators, India had overtaken Japan as the world’s fourth-biggest economy. This was to become economic fact once the Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation updated how it calculates gdp. So in one sense, the new numbers released on February 27th are a disappointment: gdp was 3.3% smaller than previously thought. In other ways, though, they are a cause for celebration. The methodological update, the first since 2015, reset the “base year”—which sets the weights for different parts of the economy—to 2022. It also added new data sources that capture a clearer picture of the Indian economy. The country looks more rural than before. Agriculture, responsible for 18% of gdp, appears bigger, largely thanks to more detail on fisheries and dairy. Finance and business services also produced a bit more output, while commerce, hotels and transport generated 26% less. The net effect is a service sector that looks 8% smaller than it did using the previous methodology, and makes up 41% of the economy. Manufacturing, which accounts for 15%, has also shrunk slightly. On the bright side, India is growing even faster than previously believed. gdp expanded by 7.1% in the fiscal year 2024-25, up from an earlier figure of 6.5%. Other numbers show it has grown quickly since, despite facing high duties on exports to America from August until last month, when the Supreme Court curtailed Donald Trump’s willy-nilly tariffing. Although manufacturing’s share failed to meet Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s goal of a quarter of gdp by 2025, high-tech production and electronics assembly are fuelling growth. Business-friendly reforms to taxes and regulations are starting to pay off, too. A qualified win for India, then—and also a victory for Indian statistics. Economists have raised doubts over the gdp figures released shortly after Mr Modi came into power 12 years ago, and which revised down growth under the previous administration. The shelving of a survey in 2019 that showed a drop in rural consumption hinted that the government might suppress inconvenient facts. That the new figures show a less rosy picture of Mr Modi’s record should reassure observers that the government will not hide unflattering data. And the figures should reassure Mr Modi that India’s title as the world’s fastest-growing big economy remains secure, even if it is not quite as big as he hoped. ■
